The Calibration Question

When a prediction market prices an event at 70%, it's making a specific claim: this event should happen roughly seven times out of ten. But is that actually true?

Calibration measures whether stated probabilities match observed frequencies. A well-calibrated market doesn't just get individual predictions right—it gets the distribution right.

We tested this by grouping hundreds of resolved predictions into probability buckets and comparing market prices against actual outcomes.

The Longshot Problem

In traditional betting markets, a well-documented phenomenon exists: people systematically overbet longshots and underbet favorites. Do prediction markets suffer the same bias?

We measured bias as the difference between predicted probability and actual frequency. Positive bias means the market overprices events (thinks they're more likely than they are).

The markets are remarkably good—but not infallible.

Statistical tests confirm these platforms are well-calibrated overall. But subtle biases exist: longshots carry a small premium, and mid-range probabilities show mild overconfidence. The edge is too small to exploit profitably, confirming what economists have long suspected—crowds aggregate information efficiently.
